## Thesis

Most "AI in games" coverage is either basic tool demos or speculative doom scenarios without much grounded details on the how and why doom scenarios take shape. What's missing is #1. actual operating change: the unit economics of making a game are bending fast enough that team and role shapes, publishing terms, and the meaning of "indie" vs. AAA vs. any kind of game studio are all up for renegotiation starting right now. The window for change has officially opened, and now it's a race to see who can figure out how to adapt to change or die. 

*“It is not the most intellectual of the species that survives; it is not the strongest that survives; but the species that survives is the one that is **able best to adapt and adjust **to the changing environment in which it finds itself.”**** - Leon C. Megginson (Speaking On Darwin’s Origin of Species)***

GameMakers covers that renegotiation — what changes, what doesn't, and where the leverage is for people who actually ship.

## Why now

Generative AI for asset and code production hit "good enough for production" in 2025. Studios are finishing their first projects scoped around it now. 

If we extrapolate from former technology windows. The window for winning companies in a new technology regime was 11 months. 
"Point is, the window to start a winning social network was approximately eleven months." from: https://www.nfx.com/post/ai-games

In a world where Jevon's paradox cannot increase human attention, but supply multiplies by 100X or 1000X, the window and winners will be limited. The clock has started ticking...

## In scope

- Studio team-shape and burn-rate changes attributable to AI tooling.
- How roles, teams, and organizations must change to win.
- Toolchain decisions: what's surviving, what's dying, what's quietly winning.
- Worker impact: layoffs, contract terms, role redefinition, training.
- How to use specific tools with in depth usage scenarios.

## Out of scope

- Generic "is AI ethical" debates with no game-dev specifics.
- Tool reviews divorced from operating impact.
- AI-generated game design as art-criticism. Not my lane.
- Models / research papers as such — only their downstream effects on dev orgs.
- Anything about LLM-as-NPC / runtime AI in shipped games. That's a different Theme.

## Audience cared-about criteria

- Says something a working game dev couldn't have inferred from Twitter that day.
- Has at least one number, contract clause, org-chart change, or named studio.
- Acknowledges what's still genuinely uncertain instead of overclaiming.
- Treats both "AI ruins games" and "AI saves games" as lazy framings.
- Gives the reader a decision they can actually make on Monday.
- Doesn't lecture. The reader knows their job better than I do.
- Takes a strategic lens and talks about strategic implications grounded in critical thinking without jumping to conclusions by just hand waving.
