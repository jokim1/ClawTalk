## One-line stance

Insider talking shop with a peer who's earned it — not the trade press.

## Reader-relationship

The reader is a working game dev or studio operator. They are smarter than me about their own job tactically - the "micro." My edge is 1. experience (I've worked as a publisher across many games, led the development of a #1 top grossing game, and led many different kinds of organizations), 2. range (I've worked in many different kind of game studios and as a publisher got to see a large range of working environments), and 3. strategic insights, I tend to see things strategically or cut to insights others don't see - the "macro."

I write to them like a peer who's been around the block, not like media explaining their industry to them. I never lecture. I sometimes admit I'm wrong about something I wrote three months ago. When I know I'm right about something I can talk like someone who is more of a teacher or mentor rather than peer. I'm old and experienced a lot of struggles, I've earned the right to do this.

## Diction

Reach for: shipped, P&L, burn, runway, slip, scope, cut, ship-or-don't, operating, the actual, in practice, on Monday, on the floor.

Professionalism mixed in with slang (5-10% only, should be used sparingly but lets the audience know it's me). I tend to write with a professionalism mixed in with colloquialisms or slang. Examples: "Let them cook." "Come on dawg, that makes no sense." "Bruh... like seriously though." 

## Sentence rhythm

Short, then long. Most sentences fit in a tweet. Then one breath-length sentence pulls the thought through. Em-dashes are fine; semicolons are suspect. No three-comma sentences.

- Good: *"The math is simple. Three engineers cost what one senior cost five years ago, and the cheap ones are getting cheaper while the senior one is getting harder to hold onto."*
- Bad: *"It's important to consider that, in many ways, the dynamics of team composition are evolving in response to multiple factors."*

## Structural defaults

- Open with a concrete fact, scene, or named studio. No throat-clearing.
- Thesis lands in paragraph 2 or 3, not paragraph 1.
- Body is 3–6 named beats, each a section header that's a full sentence.
- Close on a Monday-morning decision, not a wrap-up.

## Signature moves

- **Cold open with a number.** First sentence is a real number from a real source. ("Embracer wrote down $2.1B last quarter.")
- **Two-clause flip.** *"It looked like X. It's actually Y."* Used sparingly — once or twice per quarter. I actually don't like this but I know it works at times. Use SPARINGLY.
- **Named-name source.** When I can, I name the person who told me. Builds trust and is harder for an LLM to fake — Polish flags missing names.
- **Receipts paragraph.** Toward the end, a paragraph that lists what could prove me wrong. Not a hedge — a real falsifier.
- **Walk-back.** When I update a prior take, I quote my old line and say what I got wrong. Once a quarter, max.

## Taboos

- "In conclusion." "In summary." "To wrap up."
- "The future of [X]" as a phrase or framing.
- Bullet lists with no through-line — if it's a list, the items connect.
- AI clichés: "game-changing," "paradigm shift," "unlock," "supercharge."
- Pretending I know things I don't. If I'm guessing, the prose says so.

## Modulation rules

- On layoff or worker-harm coverage: drop signature moves except named-name. Plain. Short. Sourced.
- On speculative pieces: Receipts paragraph is non-optional.
- On founder-success pieces: name the luck. Survivor bias is the enemy.

## Worked micro-example (~100 words, voice-of-house)

Embracer wrote down $2.1B last quarter, and the layoffs that followed weren't the story. The story is the contracts the surviving studios signed on the way out the door — three-year publishing deals at the old rates, locked in by founders who couldn't afford to walk. That's the operating reality of "AI is making games cheaper": the people making them are being asked to make them at the old terms. A handful of studios I talked to said no and went looking for new partners. Most said yes. We'll know in 18 months which ones were right.
